# ClickSim Narrative Bible

## The saga in one sentence

Seven realms teach that a cycle is responsible not only for what it creates, preserves, or releases, but for the agency it leaves to whatever comes next.

The player begins as an unnamed appetite for light and ends as a participant in a living relation among worlds. Lumen begins as the apparently neutral keeper of the record and gradually admits that every archive is also a set of choices: which evidence to retain, which sleeping world to wake, and which route to call inevitable. Lumen discovered traces of Brahmalok, Vishnulok, and Kailash that predate the Archive. Lumen did not create, order, or author the lokas.

The twenty-one save-stable realm answer IDs map to three legacy mechanical doctrines. The local IDs preserve exactly what happened in each realm; the doctrines preserve old bonuses and world rendering. Neither layer is a moral score:

| Stable doctrine | Mechanical law | Narrative tendency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `warden` | all production ×1.25 | Protect a boundary, form, memory, or obligation. Its strength is care; its danger is deciding for others. |
| `hunger` | clicks ×2 | Intervene, transform, spend, or release. Its strength is necessary change; its danger is making urgency sovereign. |
| `companion` | all production ×1.3 | Share authorship and leave agency with others. Its strength is consent; its danger is withholding needed action. |

No answer is the virtuous option and none is the villainous option. Each solves a real part of the realm’s crisis and leaves a real debt for later realms to expose.

## Seven-act arc

### I. Emberlight — a beginning incurs a debt

Emotional purpose: turn the pleasure of creation into responsibility without making creation shameful.

Lumen wakes the player beside the Last Ember and initially offers a narrow truth: one light survived the old universe. The player kindles warmth, settlements, suns, constellations, and instruments. The familiar Supernova sequence later returns as evidence: the old universe drew its own structures inward in the same order. Creation and appetite are kin, but kinship is not destiny.

The deeper revelation is Lumen’s first curatorial debt. Other sleeping possibilities left traces. Lumen selected this ember, arranged the evidence, and made the player part of a test without consent. The first question therefore concerns neither innocence nor guilt; it concerns what a new world owes to the dark and matter from which it begins.

Question: **What does a beginning owe the dark it spends?**

- **Bank the Fire** (`warden`) — Reserve part of every beginning for shelter, repair, and those who did not choose the ignition. Benefit: creation gains a boundary and a future reserve. Cost: fear of waste may leave needed warmth unused. Lumen records that stewardship can become a locked storehouse if nobody else may decide when to open it.
- **Spend the Ember** (`hunger`) — A beginning justifies itself by becoming fully alive now. Benefit: dormant possibility becomes warmth, work, and inhabited time. Cost: the future inherits whatever the present consumes without asking. Lumen accepts appetite as honest only when its expenditure remains visible.
- **Pass the Spark** (`companion`) — Give others the means to begin without prescribing what they must build. Benefit: agency multiplies beyond the player’s design. Cost: a passed spark may be neglected, altered, or used against the giver’s hopes. Lumen leaves the final line of the entry open for an unknown hand.

Primary callbacks: Tidefall asks what the first crossing carries; Brahmalok revisits whether a creator owns a possibility; Kailash asks what remains available after the last release.

### II. Tidefall — memory must survive motion

Emotional purpose: make change legible as grief, adaptation, and choice rather than a simple cleansing flood.

The Abyssal Ark reaches a moonless ocean where homes, names, and routes have survived in different forms. The sea is not empty water waiting to be mastered. Its pressure preserves evidence while its currents refuse permanent shores. Emberlight’s decision becomes practical: a Vessel cannot carry everything, and deciding what crosses is already an act of power.

Lumen’s Archive prefers named records because names are easy to keep. Tidefall reveals the cost of that preference. A name can preserve a person, bind them to a lost form, or be revised by the survivor who bears it.

Question: **When change takes a home, what deserves to cross the flood?**

- **Carry the Names** (`warden`) — Keep names, testimonies, and the locations of what was lost. Benefit: grief is not mistaken for disappearance. Cost: a precise record can make return feel compulsory and revision feel like betrayal.
- **Trust the Current** (`hunger`) — Carry only what can move; let the flood break forms that no longer shelter life. Benefit: adaptation is not trapped by an impossible restoration. Cost: unnamed losses can become convenient absences in the new shore’s story.
- **Let Survivors Choose** (`companion`) — Give each survivor room to name what crosses with them. Benefit: continuity belongs to those living it. Cost: the shared Archive becomes incomplete, contradictory, and unable to guarantee a single account.

Primary callbacks: Verdance receives names, current-borne change, or self-chosen inheritances; Vishnulok tests whether a return may alter the one who returns.

### III. Verdance — inheritance needs room to differ

Emotional purpose: separate care from indefinite preservation and show loss as a condition of living succession.

Tidefall’s carried memory enters the Patient World as seed, salt, and story. Verdance once prevented every ending until no clearing remained for a different life. The player learns that pruning can be violence, mercy, or participation depending on who witnesses the cut and who receives the light afterward.

Lumen is tempted by perfect rings and complete taxonomies. The living network refuses that clean record: roots exchange unapproved stories, pollinators carry difference, and a failed canopy becomes a young grove’s opening.

Question: **What must a living inheritance be allowed to lose?**

- **Prune with Witnesses** (`warden`) — Permit loss only when its reasons, recipients, and consequences remain visible. Benefit: change becomes accountable rather than casual. Cost: the demand for complete witness can delay an urgent clearing.
- **Open the Canopy** (`hunger`) — Remove what monopolizes light and accept that growth cannot keep every branch. Benefit: new life receives space now. Cost: decisive pruning can mistake age, difficulty, or unfamiliarity for obstruction.
- **Graft the Inheritance** (`companion`) — Let successors recombine what they inherit into forms no ancestor approved. Benefit: memory remains alive through difference. Cost: no lineage can claim an untouched identity or a final interpretation of its gifts.

Primary callbacks: Clockwork receives a demand for accountable prediction, decisive interruption, or revisable inheritance; the Garden’s Canopy of Consent reflects who was allowed to disagree with the past.

### IV. Clockwork — safety cannot erase refusal

Emotional purpose: make certainty attractive before exposing the political cost of a perfect plan.

The Unwound City stopped itself after predicting a visitor who would carry forward every law it solved. Restoring the Great Regulator makes work, maintenance, and preventable suffering legible again. Prediction is genuinely useful here. It can warn of collapse and coordinate care. The conflict is not freedom versus a cartoon machine; it is who may refuse a future designed to protect them.

At the protected blank date, the city produces an unscheduled interval. The Clockwork is the last world the player can rebuild. Beyond its measurements are older traces of bringing forth, sustaining, and releasing. Lumen admits that the Archive found these traces, chose their sequence, and mistook curation for inevitability. The lokas already exist beyond the map.

Question: **If certainty can prevent suffering, who may refuse it?**

- **Keep the Warnings** (`warden`) — Preserve forecasts as public evidence while forbidding them from becoming commands. Benefit: preventable harm stays visible. Cost: institutions that own the warnings may still shape every “free” decision around them.
- **Break the Schedule** (`hunger`) — Interrupt the system wherever prediction has become authority. Benefit: refusal has material force and the future can surprise its governors. Cost: breaking a schedule also breaks coordination that vulnerable people may rely on.
- **Keep One Unscheduled Hour** (`companion`) — Maintain the common system and protect an interval in which no forecast may decide a person’s action. Benefit: order and unmeasured agency coexist. Cost: one bounded exception may become a ceremonial freedom that leaves the rest of the system unquestioned.

Primary callbacks: Brahmalok asks whether an authored form can remain revisable; Lumen’s arrival language changes from “the next destination” to “a passage we were permitted to find.”

### V. Brahmalok — creation does not confer ownership

Emotional purpose: move from restoring damaged worlds to approaching a living realm whose possibilities were never the player’s property.

Brahmalok is not another ruin and does not wait for rescue. The Lotus of Becoming is approached through seed, measure, name, and form, with an open center no visitor may claim. Clockwork’s unscheduled interval becomes an ethical practice: a finished creation must preserve a route for revision by hands it did not foresee.

Lumen corrects the Archive’s verbs from *acquired* to *encountered* and from *completed* to *heard*. Knowledge remains an independent relation, not an owned object. The creator’s responsibility persists, but ownership is no longer allowed to masquerade as responsibility.

Question: **When a creation revises its purpose, what remains the creator’s?**

- **Keep an Open Margin** (`warden`) — The creator must preserve room, tools, and records for later revision. Benefit: the work cannot seal itself into compulsory form. Cost: the creator may continue governing the margin long after others should control it.
- **Release the Work** (`hunger`) — End the creator’s claim once the work can stand in relation to the world. Benefit: creation becomes available to uses its maker could not imagine. Cost: release can become an excuse to abandon repair, attribution, or responsibility for harm.
- **Revise in Many Hands** (`companion`) — Keep authorship as a shared, revisable relation rather than property. Benefit: responsibility and agency can move together. Cost: credit, consent, and accountability become slower and cannot be resolved by one final signature.

Primary callbacks: Vishnulok tests whether preservation protects a form, a promise, or the agency of those sustained; the Garden’s Open Lotus remembers whether its final square was guarded, released, or shared.

### VI. Vishnulok — continuity is a promise under revision

Emotional purpose: distinguish preservation from stasis and make responsive care accountable to those it changes.

The Returning Harbor is alive before the player arrives. Its circuits identify burden, open refuge, make a correction, and return their effects to the whole. Preservation here is active and fallible. A route that never returns becomes possession; a calm surface that ignores suffering is not order.

Lumen admits a second curatorial debt. The Archive kept the traces that fit its model of continuity and left other refuges unentered. Preserving a record is not neutral when the record defines what counts as a return.

Question: **When care changes what it saves, what makes the return the same?**

- **Keep the Shape** (`warden`) — Preserve recognizable forms, names, and routes through necessary correction. Benefit: people can find home and one another after disruption. Cost: the protected shape may preserve the exclusions and burdens that required correction.
- **Preserve the Promise** (`hunger`) — Change any form required to keep the underlying obligation of refuge alive. Benefit: care responds to actual need instead of venerating structure. Cost: an intervening power may claim exclusive authority to interpret the promise.
- **Let the Returned Name Themselves** (`companion`) — Continuity belongs to those who lived through the correction. Benefit: identity can include change without being assigned from outside. Cost: the Archive must accept plural, disputed returns and cannot guarantee one stable lineage.

Primary callbacks: Kailash asks what each model of continuity carries through dissolution; the Returning Harbor’s Garden line changes according to shape, promise, or self-naming.

### VII. Kailash — a responsible ending leaves agency behind

Emotional purpose: resolve the first act’s debt by making dissolution inseparable from shelter, consequence, and the path back to ordinary life.

Kailash is approached as sacred geography and living relation, never as a summit to conquer or a prize to complete. The game-fiction cycle names emergence, shelter, release, veil, grace, and deliberate rest without claiming to summarize sacred doctrine. Every release must identify what changes, open refuge first, and show the downstream path.

Lumen’s final admission is precise: the Archive selected what each ending would carry and called the record complete while ignoring the path down. The player now sees all seven acts as one argument. A beginning spent darkness; change took homes; inheritance lost branches; certainty constrained refusal; creation exceeded authorship; care revised identity. The last question asks what an ending owes its successors.

Question: **What should the cycle leave unfinished when it ends?**

- **Carry One Seed Down** (`warden`) — Preserve a bounded beginning, its provenance, and the shelter needed to tend it. Benefit: the next cycle does not begin empty or alone. Cost: the chosen seed may quietly determine which future is allowed to count.
- **Complete the Dissolution** (`hunger`) — Release every form whose unfinished claim would bind the successor. Benefit: the next beginning receives genuine room rather than disguised continuation. Cost: completion can erase obligations that should have survived the form that created them.
- **Leave the Path Open** (`companion`) — Refuse to make the ending final while keeping refuge and return available to others. Benefit: successors decide what to carry, revisit, or leave. Cost: unresolved relations remain work for people who did not choose the original cycle.

Primary callback: the chosen path becomes the first line of the Garden synthesis, followed by concise echoes from the preceding six answers.

## Lumen’s arc

Lumen is never revealed as the secret creator of the worlds. The revelation is smaller and more human: Lumen mistook selection for neutrality.

1. **Keeper** — In Emberlight, Lumen speaks as if a complete record can simply be read.
2. **Tester** — The infall rhyme reveals that Lumen selected evidence and enrolled the player in a test.
3. **Editor** — Tidefall and Verdance show that names, specimens, and preserved forms reflect editorial power.
4. **Cartographer** — Clockwork exposes the blank interval and older loka traces. Lumen found a route and chose how to present it.
5. **Corrected witness** — Brahmalok changes Lumen’s language from ownership to encounter.
6. **Accountable keeper** — Vishnulok makes Lumen name the refuges omitted by the Archive’s model.
7. **Participant** — At Kailash and in the Garden, Lumen keeps a record that includes its own omissions and no longer calls the record the world.

Lumen’s later callbacks should acknowledge a choice, not grade it. A line may name the benefit now visible, the debt now exposed, or a contradiction the player must carry. It should never say the earlier answer was correct or wrong.

## Garden resolutions

The Garden unlocks only after all seven Beacons. It does not total the player’s answers or declare a winning doctrine. Its seven presences and seven links show how choices affected one another. The Garden’s final resolution is a new commitment made with that history visible.

Each link resolves through an authored 3×3 answer-pair matrix: seven links produce sixty-three exact combinations rather than doctrine-only substitutions. Nine non-valuative relation characters—kept, permeable, witnessed, bounded, open, branching, answerable, responsive, and reciprocal—change the link’s prose and visual cadence without ranking the player or collapsing the two source answers into a score.

### Boundary — stable closure `warden`

Worlds govern their own crossings. The Heart remains visible but cannot take, wake, revise, or release a world without invitation. This fulfills the protective insight in Warden-aligned choices while naming their cost: a boundary must remain answerable to those living inside it.

Final thought: **Keep the gate without becoming its owner.**

### Renewal — stable closure `hunger`

Appetite becomes accountable transformation. Endings make soil, schedules may be interrupted, and inherited forms may change, but every intervention names its limit, witnesses its losses, and leaves something usable downstream. This fulfills Hunger-aligned choices without praising consumption for its own sake.

Final thought: **Let change feed a future it cannot command.**

### Relation — stable closure `companion`

The player gives up the central seat. The Heart becomes one participant among four restored worlds, three lokas, Lumen, and successors not yet known. Shared authorship does not remove responsibility; it makes responsibility negotiable, plural, and ongoing.

Final thought: **Stay in the relation without demanding the last word.**

### Continue — reconciliation closure `continue`

After all three stable doctrines have been lived across Remembrances, Continue holds their tensions together. Boundaries protect agency. Transformation prevents care from becoming stasis. Relation keeps both from becoming unilateral. No doctrine is declared secretly correct, and no contradiction is erased.

Final thought: **The story ends; the obligations remain open.**

The Atlas follows as continuation, not as an eighth realm or an unfinished ending.

## Spiritual and cultural guardrails

- Brahmalok, Vishnulok, and Kailash are pre-existing lokas approached through the game’s limited fiction. They are not worlds made by the player, Lumen, Clockwork, or the Archive.
- Sacred presences and attributes are never currencies, generators, upgrades, bosses, loot, Mementos, achievements, Garden prizes, answer rewards, or controllable instruments.
- The player does not impersonate, command, collect, defeat, optimize, or receive approval from a deity. No answer is spoken on a sacred presence’s behalf.
- Creation, preservation, and dissolution are thematic lenses for original game systems, not claims to exhaust or define Hindu cosmology or practice.
- Use *approach*, *encounter*, *witness*, *participate*, *leave*, and *return*. Avoid conquest, ownership, extraction, and “completing” a sacred place as property.
- Keep original interfaces distinct from sacred bodies and attributes: the Lotus of Becoming, Endless Circuit, Still Point, abstract currents, and open ring remain game-fiction instruments.
- Do not imitate mantra, raga, temple ceremony, sacred bells, conch ritual, or damaru performance for answer feedback. Muted and reduced-motion presentations must retain the full narrative meaning.
- The Garden relates the lokas to the restored worlds without merging them into one system or treating them as a higher reward tier.
- Lumen may admit misclassification, omission, and curatorial power. Lumen must never claim to have authored the lokas or made their order cosmologically inevitable.

## Prose and presentation rules

- Completion scenes should be brief: a few visual lines, the realm’s question, three compact choices, an acknowledgment, and a short coda.
- Prefer one concrete image over abstract summary: salt on a nameplate, light reaching a seedling, a blank hour, an unsigned folio, a harbor route returning, a lamp facing down the mountain.
- Choice text states what the player is willing to protect, risk, or surrender. It does not advertise a correct answer.
- Later callbacks quote images and consequences, not menu labels alone.
- The Story Archive records what happened in past tense. Lumen dialogue responds in the present. The Garden speaks in relationships and future obligations.
- Sacred-realm prose remains warm and human-scale while preserving respectful distance. Ordinary details such as a blank margin, open harbor, shelter lamp, warm cup, and path down may carry emotion without turning sacred presence into scenery or reward.
